44 Days That Changed Labor

The Flint Sit-Down Strike of 1936-37

89 YEARS AGO

On December 30, 1936, auto workers in Flint, Michigan took the bold step that would transform American labor relations forever: they stopped working, sat down at their machines, and refused to leave.

For 44 days, these ordinary workers occupied GM factories in a standoff that would lead to the recognition of the United Auto Workers union and establish the fundamental right to collective bargaining in America.

Surprising Facts About the Strike

Women's "Emergency Brigade"

The strike's secret weapon was the Women's Emergency Brigade - wives, mothers and sisters who formed a militant group breaking windows to provide air when police tear-gassed the factories.

Strike "University"

Strikers organized classes inside the occupied factories, teaching labor history, public speaking, and organizing tactics, turning the occupation into an impromptu labor university.

Musical Revolution

Workers communicated and boosted morale through song. The strike birthed dozens of labor anthems that spread nationwide, creating a musical movement that influenced folk music for generations.

The Governor Who Refused

Michigan's newly elected Governor Frank Murphy refused to send in National Guard troops to forcibly remove strikers, defying immense pressure from GM and business leaders.

Global Inspiration

The sit-down tactic spread globally, inspiring workers in France, Poland and other countries to use the same strategy for gaining labor rights.
