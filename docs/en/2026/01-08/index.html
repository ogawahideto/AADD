On January 8, 1547, in the Prussian city of Königsberg, a revolutionary act took place: the printing of "Catechismusa prasty szadei" (Simple Words of Catechism) — the first book ever published in Lithuanian.

But this was more than just a book. It was an act of cultural resistance that would help preserve a language and identity through centuries of oppression.

The Man Behind the Words

Martynas Mažvydas (c. 1510-1563)

Martynas Mažvydas wasn't born to privilege. As a young Lithuanian priest in a time of religious reformation, he fled persecution to Königsberg (now Kaliningrad, Russia).

Studying at the newly established Albertina University, Mažvydas had a radical idea: to bring literacy to ordinary Lithuanian people by publishing religious texts in their native language.

At a time when Lithuanian was dismissed as a "peasant tongue" with no literary tradition, this was revolutionary.

A Language Under Threat

In the 16th century, the Lithuanian language faced extinction as Polish became the language of nobility and governance, German dominated commerce, and Latin was used by the church.

Under these pressures, Lithuanian survived primarily as an oral language among peasants and common people.

Did You Know?

Lithuanian is one of the oldest living Indo-European languages and the one closest to ancient Sanskrit. Some linguists call it a "fossil language" because it preserves sounds and grammatical forms that have disappeared from other languages.

Mažvydas' book arrived at a critical moment. Without written texts, Lithuanian might have joined the list of extinct European languages like Prussian, Polabian, and Gothic.
