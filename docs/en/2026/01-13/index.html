Sicut Dudum

The Forgotten Decree Against Slavery

591 Years • January 13, 1435

What was Sicut Dudum?

On January 13, 1435, Pope Eugene IV issued a papal bull called "Sicut Dudum" that explicitly condemned and prohibited the enslavement of the Guanche people, native inhabitants of the Canary Islands, particularly those who had converted or were converting to Christianity.

This largely forgotten decree came decades before Europeans would begin widespread colonization of the Americas, representing one of the earliest official Church positions against indigenous enslavement.

The Human Cost

"These people are living in freedom in their own homeland. By what right do you make them slaves?"

— Attributed to Canarian defenders questioning Spanish captors

The Guanche people had inhabited the Canary Islands for over a thousand years before European arrival. They lived in cave dwellings and stone houses, developed agricultural systems, and had their own religious practices.

When European explorers reached the islands in the early 1400s, they began capturing Guanches to sell as slaves in European markets. Families were torn apart. Countless people were forcibly removed from their homeland. The population was devastated not only by warfare but by diseases brought by Europeans.

The islands became a testing ground for colonial practices that would later be implemented across the Americas, making Sicut Dudum's prohibition all the more significant.

Why This Matters Today

Early Recognition

Sicut Dudum came 58 years before Columbus's voyage to the Americas and stands as one of the earliest European official recognitions of indigenous rights.

Limited Impact

Despite the decree, slavery and conquest continued. The gap between proclamation and practice reveals how economic interests often trumped moral and religious considerations.

Forgotten History

The fact that this decree is rarely discussed in history classes highlights how selective our historical memory can be, often overlooking early attempts to resist injustice.

The Legacy

Today, the Guanche culture has been largely lost, though genetic studies show that modern Canarians carry substantial Guanche ancestry. The islands themselves became a crucial stopping point for Spanish ships heading to the Americas, directly connecting the fate of the Canary Islands to the larger story of colonization.

Sicut Dudum represents a road not taken—a moment when European powers had the opportunity to establish different relationships with indigenous peoples they encountered. Its limited enforcement foreshadowed the contradictions that would characterize European colonization for centuries to come.

As we commemorate the 591st anniversary of this decree, we're reminded that history is rarely a straight line of progress, but rather a complex web of competing voices, interests, and possibilities.
