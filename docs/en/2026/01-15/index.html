The Unlikely Queen

Elizabeth Tudor was never supposed to be queen. Born to Henry VIII and Anne Boleyn, she was declared illegitimate after her mother's execution when she was just two years old.

By 1559, England had endured religious turmoil, economic crisis, and the loss of its last continental territory. The previous monarch, Mary I, had burned hundreds of Protestants at the stake, earning the nickname "Bloody Mary."

When Elizabeth inherited the crown, England was:

  • Nearly bankrupt from failed wars
  • Torn apart by religious conflict
  • Vulnerable to invasion by France and Spain
  • Suffering from failed harvests and inflation

Few believed a woman could rule effectively. Elizabeth would prove them wrong.

The Coronation: January 15, 1559

The Day's Events

The Procession

Elizabeth rode through London streets lined with cheering crowds. The road was covered in gravel and blue cloth. People threw flowers and coins, shouting blessings.

Unlike previous monarchs who kept distant, Elizabeth stopped repeatedly to thank the common people, establishing her image as "the people's queen."

The Recognition

Inside Westminster Abbey, Elizabeth was presented to the people in all four directions, with the crowd shouting "Yea, yea!" to acknowledge her as rightful queen.

The ceremony was conducted partly in English rather than Latin—a bold statement about religious reform that Elizabeth was carefully planning.

The Anointing

The most sacred part of the ceremony: Elizabeth was anointed with holy oil on her hands, breast, and forehead, symbolizing her divine appointment.

Elizabeth insisted on being anointed as kings were, with the same sacred oil, breaking with tradition for female monarchs.

The Crowning

The Archbishop placed St. Edward's Crown on Elizabeth's head. Trumpets blared, and the peers of England put on their coronets in unison.

The heavy crown was so uncomfortable that Elizabeth later joked she needed to stop the ceremony to let her neck rest!

The Banquet

A lavish feast followed at Westminster Hall, with Elizabeth seated beneath a canopy of estate. The champion of England rode into the hall and threw down his gauntlet, challenging anyone who denied Elizabeth's right to rule.

The banquet was so expensive it nearly bankrupted the already struggling treasury, but Elizabeth knew the importance of spectacle in securing her position.

Five Things You Never Knew About Elizabeth's Coronation

1. The Missing Bishop

All but one of England's bishops refused to crown Elizabeth because they were loyal Catholics. The Bishop of Carlisle finally agreed, but couldn't perform the full ceremony due to his lower rank.

2. The Secret Protestant

During the ceremony, Elizabeth quietly whispered to her ladies that the communion bread and wine were just symbols, revealing her Protestant leanings even as she outwardly followed Catholic rituals.

3. The Married Queen?

Elizabeth received a wedding ring during her coronation and referred to England as her husband, beginning her lifelong claim to be "married to the realm" rather than any mortal man.

4. The Royal Scent

The streets were fumigated with perfumes and incense for Elizabeth's procession—not just for ceremony but to combat the notorious stench of Tudor London.

5. The Tudor Superstition

Elizabeth scheduled her coronation based on astrological calculations for the most favorable date. Her father Henry VIII had done the same, as the Tudors were deeply superstitious.
